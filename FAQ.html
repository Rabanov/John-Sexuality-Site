Frequently Asked Questions

Q1: What Does Participation Entail?

First, we’ll ask you and your partner to complete a longer background survey, consisting of a number of measures to capture elements of your personality and dynamics of your relationship that we are interested in.
Then, each day, for the next 30 days, we’ll ask that you complete a much shorter daily survey, that should take you 5 minutes or less to complete. Don’t worry about having to remember—we’ll send you a prompt each day to complete the daily survey.
Lastly, we’ll ask you to place an iPod Touch in your bedroom, close to your bed (e.g., on a bed-side table); this iPod will be outfitted with psychological research software to capture audio around it. Beforehand, we’ll ask you and your partner at what time you normally retreat to your bedroom on a typical night, and program the iPod to automatically activate recording during this time, and deactivate afterwards—so you won’t need to do a thing. After 30 days, we’ll return to collect the iPod, and your participation in the study will be complete.

Q2: What Are The Aspects Of Couples’ Audio Recordings That You Are Interested In?

Almost all of them that you could think of—we’ll pass on the sounds of you and your partner snoring though! But otherwise, there is a lot of interesting and rich information to be gleaned from audio recordings of couples. We’re interested in everything, from the big (e.g., what you talk about) to the small (e.g., the physical qualities of the audio, like pitch and tempo). Whether you and your partner are merely talking about how your days went, resolving a conflict, or even engaging in sexual activity, we think there is a lot that can be learned from analyzing the audio of relationship interactions in their ‘natural environment’.

Q3: Has Anyone Done An Audio Recording Study Like This Before?

Since around the year 2000, recording audio has become an increasingly common method for psychologists to study environmental influences on people’s thoughts and behaviors. However, with your help and participation, we will be the first to use this methodology in the hopes of understanding romantic relationships at a deeper scientific level.

Q5: That’s Some Pretty Sensitive/Personal Information…How Will You Protect It?

This is a really important question, and we take the privacy and data security aspects of our study very seriously.
We will be transferring the audio files from your iPod Touch (and then wiping the iPod’s memory) to a password-protected computer in our lab, behind locked doors, that has its hard drive encrypted, and is disconnected from the internet—this means that it wont be able to be remotely accessed or hacked. We’ll also be creating a backup—just to be safe—of the audio data on a password-protected encrypted external drive that will also be stored in our lab under lock-and-key. Should we add additional external collaborators to our research team, they will only allow access to the data through secure and encrypted means.
We will also have a contact information list that will help us match couples’ background survey, daily-survey, and audio data. Once we have integrated all three of these data sources into one database, however, we will delete the contact information list—we’ll keep track of whose data is whose using randomized identification numbers. In other words, all identifiable paper records of you having participated, with the exception of the signed consent forms, will be deleted.
Finally, we will ask you if you are willing to let us use examples of audio, or written transcriptions of audio, as examples when giving presentations about our study, or for use in future studies. For example, some of your audio provides an excellent example of what “good relationship communication” actually sounds like; we may want to provide this example when presenting our results, or use it as part of a future study on relationship communication.
If you aren’t comfortable with us using your data in this way, you can still participate, and the only people who will review your audio recordings will be members of our research team.
And if you are comfortable with us using your audio data for examples or in subsequent studies, rest assured that we will obscure any audio containing identifiable information, and replace identifying text (e.g., your names) with suitable alternatives (e.g., pseudonyms) before using your data in this way.

Q6: Can We Skip The Audio Recording, But Still Participate In The Rest Of The Study?

Unfortunately not—the audio recording is a massive feature of what will make this study a unique contribution to the scientific literature on romantic relationships. However, our lab regularly conducts other daily-survey studies that do not have an audio recording element—click here to see what other participation opportunities may be available.

Q7: Can I Still Participate If I’m Single, Or If My Partner Doesn’t Want To Participate?

Nope, sorry! Unfortunately, we’re only interested in collecting data from folks in a romantic relationship, and only if both members of a couple are willing and interested in participating. We may allow individuals to participate in a later version of the study, but for this first investigation, we will only be recruiting both members of a romantic couple.

Q8: Why Do We Have To Be Cohabiting/Living Without Roommates/Childless To Participate?

These decisions about recruitment have largely been made because of pragmatic reasons. If you aren’t cohabiting with your partner, we aren’t going to get as much couple-related data from the audio recording as we’d like to. And the ethics of audio recording becomes much more complicated if there is the possibility that non-consenting (e.g., roommates) individuals or minors might unintentionally be recorded. Some of these recruitment decisions might change in our future studies, but for now, these recruitment decisions help to ensure that we get interesting data, but only from consenting adults.
